# 5G — Architecture, Capabilities, Reality

I'll say the unpopular thing up front: most "5G" marketing you've seen is describing SA-only features on networks that are still NSA. It's not exactly lying, it's more like a car ad showing the sport trim's top speed while you're financing the base model. Doesn't mean 5G is fake — the SA rollout is real and slicing genuinely works where it's deployed — just don't take the billboard at face value. More on that below.

## The three service categories 5G was designed for

| Category | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **eMBB** | enhanced Mobile Broadband — more speed | 4K streaming, AR/VR |
| **URLLC** | Ultra-Reliable Low-Latency Comms | remote surgery, factory robots, V2X |
| **mMTC** | massive Machine-Type Comms | millions of IoT sensors per km² |

No single deployment does all three at once well — that's what **network slicing** is for.

## Network slicing — 5G's headline feature

One physical network, sliced into multiple **virtual end-to-end networks**, each with its own SLA:

```
        ┌────────────── Physical 5G infrastructure ──────────────┐
Slice A │ eMBB   — high bandwidth   → consumer mobile broadband  │
Slice B │ URLLC  — 1ms latency      → smart factory              │
Slice C │ mMTC   — tiny, many       → utility meters             │
        └────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```
Each slice is isolated (traffic, QoS, even security). Enabled by virtualization + the service-based core. Think "multi-tenant Kubernetes namespaces" but for a national network.

## SA vs NSA — the deployment reality

- **NSA (Non-Standalone)**: 5G radio bolted onto the existing **4G core**. Faster to launch, gives speed but not the fancy features. Most early "5G" was this.
- **SA (Standalone)**: real **5G core (5GC)**. Unlocks slicing, URLLC, edge. What actually delivers on 5G's promises. Rollout is ongoing and slower/costlier.

When someone says "is this real 5G?" — they usually mean *is it SA?*

## Edge computing (MEC)

5G lets the **UPF** (user-plane) sit physically close to users — at the edge. Combined with **Multi-access Edge Computing**, apps run near the device:
- Latency drops from ~50ms to single-digit ms.
- Use cases: cloud gaming, industrial automation, autonomous vehicles, AR.
- For developers: your backend can be deployed *inside* the operator's edge, not a distant datacenter.

## The frequency story

- **Sub-6 GHz**: the workhorse. Good balance of coverage and capacity. Most 5G is here.
- **mmWave (24–47 GHz)**: gigabit speeds, but range of a city block and blocked by walls/rain. Stadiums, dense downtowns.
- Physics unchanged: no band gives both huge coverage *and* huge capacity.

## Hype vs reality (be the person who knows the difference)

| Claim | Reality |
|---|---|
| "1ms latency everywhere" | Only URLLC slices on SA, near the edge — not your phone in general |
| "10 Gbps speeds" | Peak lab / mmWave numbers; real-world is far lower but still strong |
| "5G replaces fiber" | Fixed Wireless Access is real for some homes, not a universal fiber killer |
| "Slicing is everywhere" | Needs SA core + business/OSS integration; still maturing |

## Security improvements over 4G

- **Encrypted subscriber identity**: the IMSI is no longer sent in the clear (defeats "IMSI catchers"/stingrays) — now a concealed **SUCI**.
- Stronger mutual authentication (5G-AKA).
- Service-based interfaces use TLS + OAuth2 between network functions — literally the same primitives as your web APIs ([JWT/OIDC](../cybersecurity/web-security.md)).

## Why it matters to software people

5G core is cloud-native: containers, CI/CD, service mesh, observability, APIs (see [CAMARA in oss-bss](oss-bss.md)). The gap between "telecom engineer" and "backend engineer" is closing fast.
